ANU Observer

During my final semester at ANU, I served as a graphic designer for the ANU Observer. In collaboration with the Visual Team, I produced content for the campus newspaper, utilizing Adobe Illustrator and Photoshop to create and format graphics for both online publications and social media promotion. Each week, our team leader assigned three graphic briefs via Slack, each with a two-day deadline to ensure timely news coverage. A significant aspect of my role involved presenting innovative ideas related to web design, merchandise, and social media content during our weekly team meetings. Additionally, as a member of the Visual Team, I contributed to event coverage by working backstage at campus events such as Theatre Sports and Big Night Out. My responsibilities included producing promotional materials, assisting with setup, photographing events, operating video cameras, and assisting live streaming. Throughout my experience at the Observer, I excelled in a collaborative environment, effectively meeting tight deadlines while passionately representing the Observer brand through high-quality visual content.

Think Bee

“Think Bee” is an innovative industrial microarchitecture brick designed to provide essential habitats for both burrowing and nesting bee species within urban residential environments. This project was developed in response to a custom brief focused on “reinventing the brick” as part of my capstone individual project at ANU. The initiative features a system for randomized organic microterrain generation within the brick’s structure, aiming to reintroduce natural forms and habitats into residential architecture. The final outcomes of the project included standard product renders, upscale sculptural renders, and a physical material test utilizing a fired cardboard clay material.


Logan Student Center

This work was part of my capstone group project at ANU, where we collaborated with a client to completely redesign the interior of the Griffith University Logan Student Centre. Our solution centered around the theme of community, with a strong emphasis on accessibility within the space. Our final design aimed to connect with the Logan community through the incorporation of local Grevillea flower iconography. We adopted an open-plan approach that effectively segregated eating and study areas while also accommodating the space for community events.


Air Aware

The Air Aware system was developed in response to a project brief that required us to create a socially engaged product capable of interacting with its environment through various inputs and outputs. We utilized the Arduino physical computing system in conjunction with 3D-printed components to address the pressing issue of declining air quality. Our system discreetly monitors air quality data and establishes a wireless communication network that relays this information via a dedicated website. This approach ensures effective dissemination of critical air quality information to the community.


Botanical Blocks

This project was developed in response to a student-led group brief that challenged us to address a complex, "wicked" problem. Our team focused on the issue of environmental accessibility in urban rental spaces.

Through extensive research and interviews, we discovered that renters often face challenges in owning, maintaining, and incorporating green spaces within their homes. In response, we designed the Botanical Block System—a modular, stackable vertical garden specifically created for indoor environments.

The system is highly portable, low-maintenance, and integrates seamlessly into small urban spaces, offering renters a practical solution for bringing nature into their homes.


Mycelium Chair

This chair was designed in response to a brief asking students to "reveal an unseen system." I developed a concept for a hypothetical client, Botanical Gardens Canberra, with the aim of unveiling hidden elements of the forest environment for an exhibition.

My design highlights the unseen mycelium networks that exist beneath the forest floor. The chair is composed of two key elements: the legs, which symbolize the intricate mycelium structures, and the seat, representing the mushroom cap.

Using real mycelium data, I generated 3D assets in Fusion 360 to accurately reflect these natural patterns. The seat features three leather cushions, designed for both comfort and aesthetic appeal, merging functionality with a narrative of nature's hidden systems.
Bauhaus Stool

For this brief, I was tasked with creating a unique stool design. My approach was to craft a sleek, industrial modern aesthetic, drawing inspiration from the Bauhaus movement, specifically the iconic Wassily Chair.

The form of the stool was influenced by the clean, geometric lines of a paper Z-fold, and the design evolved through a rapid prototyping process. The final product is intended to be constructed from bent steel tubing paired with a leather cushion, combining minimalist style with functional comfort.

Randwick Cycling Club Poster

This poster was designed for the Randwick Cycling Club to promote their ‘2024 Retro Day’ event. My goal was to capture a vintage cycling aesthetic, combining nostalgic elements with modern design principles.

To achieve this, I utilized simplified color blocks and a cool primary color scheme, complemented by hand-drawn graphic elements. In terms of typography, I paired a decorative serif font for the title with a light geometric font for body text, creating a balanced yet dynamic look.

The poster evolved through multiple iterations: the yellow version represents an earlier concept, while the blue version reflects the final solution, which was tailored to meet the club’s branding requirements.


Drought Ride

In 2019, I contributed to the design efforts for The Drought Ride, a charity cycling event held in Cabonne Country, New South Wales. My role involved designing the event logo and creating hat designs, which were distributed to the participants.

The event was organized to raise awareness and funds in response to the severe droughts that heavily impacted the livelihood of farmers in the region. The Drought Ride successfully raised $85,000, which was donated directly to regional community centers, providing essential support networks for struggling local farmers.


OCI Redesign

In early 2024, I completed a freelance project in collaboration with the Old Canberra Inn (OCI), a historic craft beer institution located in Canberra. The client sought to reflect the building’s history as an old highway stop, using heritage Australian color palettes to evoke a sense of nostalgia.

For the logo, I focused on emphasizing the OCI initials to appeal to a younger audience while addressing the challenge of maintaining a connection to the full name, Old Canberra Inn. I selected Century Gothic as the primary typeface, drawing inspiration from the Victorian neogothic period in which the establishment was founded, while incorporating vintage-style emphasis strokes to enhance historical ties.

In addition to the logo, I developed supporting assets, including a Gang-gang cockatoo illustration and a simplified representation of the building’s façade, further integrating the rich history of the location into the brand identity.


Pathway To CMAG

In my final semester at ANU, I collaborated with the Canberra Museum and Gallery (CMAG) to design and implement a public program. The project focused on two key themes: fostering connections within the Canberra community and improving wayfinding from Canberra Centre to the museum. We aimed to address the challenge of limited foot traffic in the area by creating an engaging and intriguing experience.

Our program featured a stall with interactive activities, where participants were invited to express how Canberra made them feel by writing down their thoughts on paper. To enhance engagement, we introduced a tactile component, encouraging visitors to represent their feelings physically. Additionally, we set up a chalk drawing station at the base of the hill and created a drawn pathway leading directly to the stall, which was located at the museum’s entrance.

The initiative was successful in attracting a diverse audience, particularly families and individuals looking for a fun, creative activity on a Saturday afternoon. This approach effectively increased foot traffic to the gallery, drawing in visitors who were encouraged to explore the exhibition spaces after participating in the outdoor activities.

Dynamic ID Redesign

The Dynamic ID project involved utilizing P5.js coding software to create a variety of visual branding assets for a custom brief. Our group chose to develop a new identity for the Canberra International Music Festival, using architectural data from concert locations to inspire interpretive designs and patterns.

This approach enabled us to produce a distinctive set of variable ticket designs that changed based on the music genre and location of each event. The result was a visually engaging ticketing system that reflected the unique character of the festival.

Additionally, I developed an unrelated infinite terrain generator using the same software, showcasing my ability to leverage coding for creative applications.


Habitecture

This group project was developed in response to a brief requiring us to design a working-scale habitat for an endangered local species, the Gang-gang cockatoo. This iconic bird is facing habitat loss due to urbanization and sprawl in the Canberra region.

Our objective was to reintegrate the Gang-gang cockatoo into urban environments by creating a nesting solution that could be attached to houses and buildings. We focused on a design that harmonized with the aesthetics of local Canberra architecture, ensuring it complemented the surrounding environment.

The final product was constructed from durable, insulating decking wood, featuring a soft interior designed specifically for nesting. This initiative aimed to promote biodiversity and support the preservation of this beloved species within the urban landscape.
GangGang Redesign

In this project, the brief tasked us with creating a new brand image for the local Downer café, Gang Gang.

My approach focused on bridging the gap between the younger crowd that frequented the café at night for live music gigs and the older audience that visited during the day. I achieved this by developing a lighthearted blend of design elements, combining 1960s-70s cartoon print techniques and photography with aesthetics and imagery from the iconic Australian punk rock scene. The result was a playful, parodic combination that appealed to both demographics, looking to foster a vibrant and inclusive atmosphere for the café.
